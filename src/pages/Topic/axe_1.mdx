"Step 12 provides a more detailed view of the evolution in La Rochelle's trade due to the loss of Canada. We compare La Rochelle's product terms networks of imports and exports before (1750-1755) and after (1765-1770) the war. If the main commodity terms that composed the network remained present throughout the two periods, their relative places changed but, more importantly, we can observe an important decrease in the diversity of commodity terms in both imports and exports (networks are less dense) after the war. By removing one important trading partner, the loss of Canada diminished the quantity and the variety of La Rochelle trade. Before the Seven Years War, La Rochelle was a mid-size international trading hub with a genuine specialization due both to its specific local productions (salt and cognac) and to its quasi-monopole on trade with Canada. La Rochelle was shipping in hides (peaux) from Canada, which it re-exported to several destinations, sometime after having transformed them, especially into hats (chapeaux). It was exporting to Canada first necessity products like iron-made objects and tools, as well as a variety of textile products including predominantly cloth products (toile). But besides these main categories, a wide range of other products, mostly linked to the West Indies trade, were exported or re-exported via La Rochelle. After 1765, the imports network was dominated by products destined for shipbuilding (commodities made of wood, iron and to a lesser extent copper) - and goods from the West Indies (sugar). The trade in hides almost disappeared and exports were dominated by a handful of colonial products. Even the international trade of La Rochelle's traditional exports, salt and brandy, was badly hit. One hypothesis is that instead of going directly to a foreign destination, these products were now directed to other French Atlantic ports (Nantes and Bordeaux) from which they were exported. After 1765, La Rochelle was no longer a significant international port and even if it regained a decent volume of trade in the 1780s, it relied almost entirely by then on the slave trade, and was financed entirely by Bordeaux and Nantes merchants [(J. #### 1.3.4.2 Des acteurs locaux pour le commerce colonial et négrier

Pour le commerce colonial, on constate que La Rochelle arme plus à la traite négrière qu'au commerce colonial. A notre connaissance, c'est le seul port négrier d'une certaine importance ayant cette spécificité. Sur 755 armements négriers français entre 1784 et 1790, 109 sont armés à La Rochelle ou à Rochefort (14.4%). En 1789, ce sont ainsi 13 négriers qui sortent de la région étudiée, contre 6 navires seulement armés en droiture pour les colonies, parmi lesquels un se rend aux Mascareignes (il n'est pas un négrier  - vérifié chez Toussaint). Tous les négriers armés à La Rochelle (10) ou à Rochefort (3) vont ensuite à Saint-Domingue (1 cas, destination inconnue). Au total, la région peut donc compter sur une 20aine de retours depuis les colonies à plantation.

Analyse faite (slavevoyages.org), ces navires négriers sont tous armés par des armateurs de la région (1 armateur est inconnu, 2 ne font que cet armement négrier là, les autres armement entre 3 et 13 négriers toujours depuis La Rochelle )
